## **Free‑surface flow measurements by non‑intrusive methods: a survey** 

## **G. Gomit[1] · L. Chatellier[1] · L. David[1]** 

Received: 12 January 2022 / Revised: 6 April 2022 / Accepted: 28 April 2022 / Published online: 26 May 2022 © The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer-Verlag GmbH Germany, part of Springer Nature 2022 

## **Abstract** 

Measurement of the free surface shape and its evolution in time and/or in space is of great interest in many engineering/ research applications. The optical measurements of a liquid interface over an extended area can be very challenging for different reasons including the fluid properties, the seeding requirements, the optical access or the dimension of the interest area. In this review, the state of the art of non-intrusive field measurement techniques is presented. The principles and implementations of the different categories of methods will be described. Advantages, limitations and drawbacks of the different families of solutions will be addressed to help the experimentalists choosing the most appropriate methodology.

Most of the applications discussed in this review concern the measurement of the air/water interface in various hydrodynamic problems including wave generated by wind or by a moving obstacle, dam break flow or wave–current interaction. Fluid surface measurements are obviously not exclusive to water. Numerous studies have applied or developed free surface measurements with other fluids in a wide range of fluid mechanics applications. physical phenomena in many engineering/research applications. The free surface elevation or gradient provided by laboratory or field experiments is also particularly useful for the development and the validation of numerical methods. 

Among the various methods for measuring a liquid surface, the capacitive waves probes and the resistive wave probes are the most frequently used devices. This kind of instrument measures the surface height evolution at a given location and is easy to implement in a laboratory flume or tank. These wave probes provide a punctual measurement and should be used in line or array configuration to access spatial information (Stewart 1980). A strong limitation of these probes remains the intrusiveness of the measurement, as the probe pierces the interface and can generate interferences with the flow. A comparison between this type of probe and a non-invasive laser slope gauge is given by Lange et al. 1982. They pointed out that the optical probe has many advantages, including the avoidance of disturbances, the ability to measure simultaneously in two component directions, and a higher maximum frequency limit. For that reason, this survey focuses on non-intrusive techniques. In a more general way, the development and the use of non-invasive methods coincides with the standardization of non-intrusive optical measurements in fluid mechanics as for example the PIV ( _Particle Image Velocimetry_ ) and LDV ( _Laser Doppler Velocimetry_ ) for velocity, the LIF (LaserInduced Fluorescence) for concentration or temperature, the BOS ( _Background Oriented Schlieren_ ) for density or droplet sizing in sprays. 

Non-intrusive surface measurement methods can be classified according to the extent of the measurement: point, line or field. For point measurements, several types of noncontact surface sensors are commonly used in hydrodynamic applications including acoustics sensor, laser sensor, pressure sensor or LIDAR ( _Light Detection And Ranging_ ). The extension of 1D measurements to line measurements often requires the use of lasers and image processing. As an example, specific two-dimensional instruments such as LiDAR can been used for surface profile measurement (Blenkinsopp et al. 2012 _)_ . Another common line measurement technique is based on planar laser-induced fluorescence (PLIF) (Duncan et al. 1999). A laser sheet is used to illuminate the surface of the water in which a fluorescent dye is added. The intersection between the laser and the interface is imaged and specific processing is used to extract the surface position. This intersection can also be extracted based on PIV images when the free surface is imaged by the camera. In this case of free surface flow images, dynamic masking algorithms have been developed to detect the interface line (Sanchis and Jensen 2011). 

In recent years, different optical methods for field surface measurements applied to fluid mechanics have been developed to measure and analyse free surface flows. The 

development of these methods has often been based on advances in laser technology and the quality of optical sensors. These techniques have the advantage of being nonintrusive while allowing instantaneous measurements of surface shape (slope or height) over extended area with high space and time resolution. In this paper, similarly to van Meerkerk et al. 2020 , the methods are classified into three categories: 

- Techniques based on stereo-correlation and presence of key features at the interface 

- Techniques based on projection of fringe or regular pattern 

- Techniques using optical properties of light through the interface and into a fluid: refraction, reflection and absorption 

The principles and implementations of each of these classes of methods are presented in detail. The choice of a free surface measurement method for a given application will depend on a large number of parameters as: the dynamics of the flow in time and space (stationary, unsteady, periodic, turbulent, wave amplitude and slope...), the properties of the liquid in particular the optical properties (transparent, opaque, refractive index, absorption...), the measurement conditions (laboratory or field measurements, scales, accessibility...) or the type of data required for the physical analysis (frequency, height, gradient, surface velocity...). To discuss this important point, advantages, limitations and drawbacks of the different families of solutions will be addressed to help the experimentalists choose their methodology. 

## **2  Stereovision based method** 

Stereoscopic methods are based on the identification of characteristic features from at least two points of view. In the field of solid mechanics, stereovision systems coupled with Digital Image Correlation (DIC) are widely used for shape and surface deformation measurements. One of the main challenges for the application of this stereoscopic methods on a fluid interface, including a water surface, is the specular properties on the surface. As explained in Jähne et al. 1994 , this specular nature of the reflections can make so that characteristic features seen in one image are not necessarily found in the other (stereoscopic matching). To solve this problem, experimenters mainly use an adapted light and surface seeding. Different approaches have been developed and used in laboratory or in the field. The differences between the methods are mainly related to the reconstruction algorithm used. Among these methods, we can cite stereoscopic matching and triangulation-based methods,

In this part, the main principles of the stereovision-based methods are introduced and a review of the implementations of these methods in the lab and/or in the field is presented. Since these methods require the presence of key features on the interface, a specific paragraph is dedicated to surface seeding solutions. 

## **2.1  Stereoscopic principle and reconstruction methods** 

Stereoscopic reconstruction methods rely on the use of at least two calibrated cameras observing the same scene. The objective of the reconstruction process is to match the 2D information from the images in order to accurately reconstruct the 3D surface. The common step to all stereoscopic methods is the calibration of the cameras. This step provides a relationship between the 3D coordinates of the scene and the camera pixels in the form of a projection equation. The reconstruction of the surface can then be achieved with different methods. In this part, two types of reconstruction approaches are developed. The first type of method relies on the used on stereo-matching algorithms and triangulation (inversion of the projection equation). The second type of reconstruction method developed here is based on _Direct Image Correlation_ (DIC) based on parametric shape and displacement which are directly projected on the camera models.

## **2.1.2  Surface reconstruction methods** 

Given images from a calibrated stereoscopic system, a large number of image processing methods can lead to the 3D reconstruction of a surface. Here, we present in detail 2 types of reconstruction methods: one based on stereoscopic matching exploiting the epipolar geometry and the other based on the generation of parametric shapes which are directly projected on the camera models. 

Many stereovision matching algorithms have been developed for various applications including robotic, computer vision or solid surface measurements. Lazaros et al. 2008 provides a review of these algorithms. The difference between the image coordinates of two corresponding points is commonly referred as disparity. As noted by Lazaros et al. 2008, all algorithms use a matching cost function to compute this disparity. The most common functions are based on the absolute intensity differences, the squared intensity differences, or based on normalized cross-correlation. In these studies, the size of the fields of view can reach up to several dozen

Because of the size of the field of view, one of the main difficulties of these applications is the calibration of the stereovision system. Another difficulty to apply stereo-correlation based method on sea surface is related to the specular reflection in the recorded images. The footprint of these reflections in the images leads to correspondence problems during stereo-matching. Several solutions have been proposed to minimize this issue. Wanek and Wu 2006 used three stereo pairs in combination with specific matching algorithm to increase the correspondence success. However, Kosnick and Dulov 2011 or Benetazzo 2006 outlined that if the angle of the stereo-system related to the mean sea level is well chosen, and if considering a small enough area of the sea surface, the reflections can be considered as texture on the water surface. 

## **2.3  Seeding choice** 

As seen previously, the use of particle seeding is necessary in most of the measurements based on stereo-correlation in the laboratory. Numerous studies in which the velocity on the free surface is estimated by SPIV or LSPIV ( _Large Scale PIV_ ) techniques provide examples of available particles including Fujita et al. 1998 or Weitbrecht et al. 2002. Weitbrecht et al. 2002 provide a comparison of several types of particles for LSPIV according to different characteristics such as the densities or the agglomeration rate. They recommend the use of polypropylene particles because of the density is slightly lower than water and because their agglomeration rate is lower than the other types of particles. Another criterion must be based on the contrast provided by the tracer with surface. In cases of field measurements, in river for example, the choice of these tracers should also take into account environmental concerns. In this context, Chandler et al. 2008 mentioned as well that the choice of appropriate tracers and methods of seeding is one of the key challenges. In this study, the author tested the feasibility and evaluated the accuracy of a digital photogrammetry system for measuring water surface elevation in a river. They used “biodegradable packing chips” consisting of starch which disintegrate within a few hours. ## **3  Technique based on the projection of diffusive pattern** 

An alternative method to the surface seeding is to project a pattern onto the liquid free surface. Depending on the surface reconstruction method, it may be a random pattern such as a speckle, a regular pattern such as a grid or a fringes pattern. In this part, we present only the methods that use the projection as a marker of the surface, i.e. that we not address the methods based on specular reflections. The latter will be discussed in part 4 of the paper. Due to the low reflectance of the water surface, the projection of a diffusive pattern as a marker is not trivial and light diffusivity is often obtained by using specific dyes and/or specific illumination systems. 

## **3.1  Projection of a diffuse pattern on a liquid surface** 

Regardless of the reconstruction method used, one of the main challenges of using projection-based methods is to obtain a diffuse pattern with minimum specular reflections and a minimum diffusivity in depth of the light rays in the 

liquid. This is trivial in the case of surface measurements on an opaque liquid such as paint (Kouyi et al. 2003) but it requires more efforts in case of water because of its low reflectivity. A common solution to this problem is to use a white dye to colour the water and obtain a diffuse surface. As mentioned by Przadka et al. 2012, the chosen dye should provide a high reflectance of the water surface without forming a film at the interface that can affect the surface tension of the water and change the attenuation properties. For example, Zhang et al. 2013 noted that a latex paint affects the surface tension of the water. Przadka et al. 2012 have also highlighted this phenomenon when water is coloured with plain paint. They tested different types of titanium dioxide particles and showed that the titanium dioxide _Anatase_ particles allow to achieve damping equivalent to the clean surface case (Fig. 6). We also note that the concentration of dye must be sufficient to avoid light penetration into the liquid. Such a diffusion phenomenon in depth can lead to an error on the measured surface as observed by Kouyi et al 2003. 

Another solution is to make the liquid fluorescent and use an adapted illumination. Typical fluorescent dye used in experimental fluids mechanics are green fluorescein or Rhodamine (6G or WT). By adding this kind of dye, techniques based on LIF (Laser Induced Fluorescence) principle have been widely used for surface line measurements (see for example Duncan et al. 1999 or Euvé et al. 2015). 3D methods based on fringes (Grant et al 1990) or lines (van Meerkerk et al. 2020) projection have also been developed and detailed in the following. ## **3.2  Projection of random pattern** 

The principle of this type of method is to project a random pattern onto the surface of the liquid observed by a stereoscopic system. Then, a stereoscopic reconstruction method 

such as those described in the first part of the paper can be used. 

Based on this approach, Tsubaki and Fujita 2005 presented a method for measuring two-dimensional water surface. Using an opaque-white dye to make “ _water surface appears like a solid surface_ ” and a LCD projector, they projected a random pattern recorded by a stereoscopic system composed of two cameras (Fig. 7). The surface reconstruction was performed with classical stereo-correlation algorithm based on 3D pinhole calibration and on epipolar geometry (see Part 4.5). The authors pointed out that the presence of specular reflections leads to errors in image matching. Indeed, unlike diffuse light, the path of the light rays from specular reflections depends on the viewing position. This reflection noise produced “bright hair-like noise” onto the images. The authors noted that this noise has specific properties and can be removed using a filtering procedure. Tsubaki and Fujita 2005 evaluated the measurement accuracy of the method on still water surface and calibrated wavy plates with error less than 1 mm over a field of view around 150*100 mm. Applying the method over wavy fluid surface, they show that the method is able to "trace the propagation of small waves as small as 1 mm" and demonstrated the robustness of the technique in a discontinuous surface with breaking wave occurring during the measurement. 

This kind of method based on the projection of random pattern is widely used in the field of solid surface measurement. Many stereo vision sensors are now available and could be used in the case of a liquid surface as long as a diffuse projection on the surface is obtained. For example, Bung et al. 2021 used a low-cost 3D sensor, also called RGB-D camera (Red Green Blue + Depth) to study the turbulent free-surface of a hydraulic jump.

## **3.3  Projection of regular pattern: dots, lines and grid**

## **4  Technique based on light properties: reflection, refraction or absorption** 

This type of method is based on the physical properties of light propagating in a medium with an optical interface. Among these methods, the refraction-based measurements are nowadays widely used in particular for their accuracy in measuring the slope and/or the height of the surface and also because their implementation is often less challenging than for other types of method (Aureli et al. 2014). Numerous studies, including Jähne et al. 1994, 2005; Zhang and Cox 1994, Tropea et al. 2007 or Moisy et al. 2009, provide an extensive state of the art of the methods. The common principle of field measurement based on refraction is the analysis of the apparent deformation of a pattern located below the surface and imaged from at least a point above the surface. Figure 13 shows the apparent deformation of a checker board when the surface is disturbed (right) compared to flat surface (left). 

The principle for field measurement based on refraction was introduced by Cox 1958 despite the fact that he measured time series of wave slope in a wind/wave at only one point. He used a collimated light beam encoded with spatially varying intensity in a way that allows a direct 

relationship between intensity and surface slope. A similar optical principle was extended by Keller and Gotwols 1983 and Jähne and Riemer 1990 who developed a system to measure two-dimensional field of one component of the surface wave slope. In this study, they used a submerged glass box filled with a suspension of particles illuminated from one side in order to generate a scattered light. This light source, with decaying intensity in the direction of the light projection, was imaged by a camera located far above the free surface so that it receives only the vertical light rays (see Fig. 14 from Tropea et al. 2007). Based on ray tracing, the light intensity received at a pixel is then encoding the surface slope. With this system, only the slope component in the direction of the light gradient can be estimated. In addition, the resulting measurement is sensitive to both slope and elevation of the surface. The advantage of using collimated light instead of scattered light, even if the optical setup is more complex, is that the measured slope is insensitive to the surface height. This principle has been used by Zhang and Cox 1994 who developed a method for measuring the two components of the surface gradient, called _Free Surface Gradient Detector_ ( _FSGD_ ). The optical system (Fig. 14) comprises a large lens and a colour screen with a suitable two-dimensional colour pattern that is located at the focal length from the lens. The camera is located far above the surface so that the rays emerging from the water surface that enter the camera are vertical. As mentioned by Jähne et al. 1994, this optical system acts as a telescope. Because the colour screen is placed in the focal plane of the lens, the rays from the surface that pass through the lens focus onto one point of the screen. Therefore, the image of the refracted colour pattern is encoding the surface gradient. With this method, a calibration of the slope-colour relation is required and is performed using a glass sphere with known refractive index. As explained previously in this section, the reflectionbased methods, as the _Free Surface Gradient Detector_ developed by Zhang and Cox 1994, appears to be limited to smaller slopes than refraction-based methods. However, few studies have presented and used optical method based on this principle. For example, Sanada et al. 2008 measured 

the wake of a ship model in a towing tank (Fig. 19). Their experiment is based on the use of a light screen, a multicolour checkerboard, located above the water surface. The water surface at rest acts as a flat mirror while the wavy water surface acts as a distorting mirror. The slope of the surface is estimated using the deformation of the pattern and a specular reflection law. A Poisson’s equation is then solved to obtain the surface shape. A similar approach has been proposed by Rupnik et al. 2015. Zappa et al. 2008 proposed another way to take advantage of light reflection on the free surface. They used the relationship between surface orientation and the change in polarization of reflected light. Based on this principle, they presented a two-dimensional slope measurements system using polarimetric images of reflected light. 

Finally, also based on reflection, a multi-view based method of specular reflections has been proposed by Kiefhaber et al. 2015. As discussed in the part about the stereoscopic method, specular reflections can complicate the matching during the stereo-process. Indeed, in case of specular reflections, the image of the surface will not depend only on the shape of the surface but also on the characteristics of the light source origin and of the camera orientation. Kiefhaber et al. 2015 developed a setup (Fig. 20), named _Reflective Stereo Slope Gauge_ , that combines stereo cameras and associated illumination to ensure that the path of the light rays entering the two cameras is the same. This configuration allows a direct wave height measurements based on stereo triangulation.

## **5  Discussions** 

In this section, the main advantages/limitations of the different categories of methods are discussed to facilitate the selection of an appropriate methodology by the experimentalist. The choice of a free surface measurement method for a given application will depend on a large number of parameters and experimental constraints. The experimentalist must answer three main questions: what data are required for the physical analysis of the studied free surface flow? What are the characteristic quantities (height, slope, time scale, wavelength) of the free surface flow? What are the constraints for the surface measurement on my experiment? Table 2 presents the main characteristics of typical methods presented in this review. 

## **5.1  Data required for the physical analysis** 

The type of data required for the physical analysis depends on the hydrodynamic applications and the foreseen use of the data. The optical methods presented in this review allow the measurement of the slope and/or the height of the free surface. In addition, methods based on the use of surface seeding can give access to the velocity estimation. 

Some methods allow to have access only to surface gradients. In particular, the methods based on refraction or reflection including the FSGD developed by Zhang and Cox 1994. This kind of method allows to detect weak slopes with application on small amplitudes waves as capillary waves or wind-generated waves (see part 3 and Moisy et al. 2009 for limitation criterion). On the same type of application, for short wind waves, combined optical slope/height measurements can be performed with the imaging technique described in Jähne et al. 2005 or Aureli et al. 2014. We also note that most refraction-based methods rely on the gradient measurement and an integration step is used to reconstruct 

the height (Moisy et al. 2009, Fouras et al. 2008). This can be a limitation if the reference height is not known and in case the absolute height of the surface is searched. In this case, other types of methods as stereo-correlation based methods (including for example Chatellier et al. 2013 or Ferreira et al. 2017); projection based method (including for example Cobelli et al. 2009 or Tsubaki and Fujita 2005) or absorption based method (Aureli et al. 2014) have to be used. Finally, if velocity data are required in combination with shape, methods based on the use of floating particles are required. StereoPIV-based methods (Chatellier et al. 2013, Turney et al. 2009) or PTV (Douxchamps et al. 2005) can then be implemented. In this case, the choice of tracers is of major concern (see part 2.3). 

The spatial and temporal resolution of the data must also be considered. Some methods, especially those based on scanning, are only applicable on stationary (Gomit et al. 2015) or slowly varying (van Meerkerk et al. 2020) flows. In these cases, the temporal scale is only limited by the available scanning and recording equipment. However, all of the presented methods, under the assumption of using high-speed cameras and obtaining sufficient lighting, can be adapted to high-speed measurements. For example, Kiefhaber et al. 2014, implemented a refraction-based method with a frequency acquisition of 1500 Hz. 

The spatial resolution of an optical method depends on the resolution of the camera, the field of view and the selected method. Some methods provide a height measurement for each camera pixel. In particular, this is the case for the methods based on the refraction of a continuous pattern (for example Koskulics et al. 2012) or the Moiré methods (Cochard et al. 2008). For methods involving correlation, e.g. stereovision with particles or projection method (Tsubaki and Fujita 2005) or refraction of random pattern (Moisy et al. 2009), the spatial resolution also depends on the size of the correlation windows. ## **5.2  Experimental constraints** 

The experimental constraints have a major role in the choice of the method. For field measurements, stereovision-based methods seem the most suitable, as for sea surface measurement (e.g. Benetazzo 2006). Similarly, for large fields of view in laboratory, stereovision-based methods are often employed (Ferreira et al. 2017). However, these measurements are also limited by the size of the facility. For example, it can be difficult to use dye or particles in large facilities where the amount of water is significant. In this case, projection-based methods (Gomit et al. 2015 ) or reflection-based methods (Sanada et al. 2008) can be used. The size of the field of view must also be taken into account in the camera calibration method. Several solutions to this particular problem have been presented in this review. 

The properties of the liquid, in particular the optical properties, are an essential point for the implementation of a method. For opaque fluids, projection methods can be easy to deploy. In water, projection-based methods require the use of dye that can modify the fluid surface properties (see part 4.1). Similarly, the experimentalist must ensure that the addition of particles on the surface or in the volume does not change the physical properties of the liquid. The refraction methods require a transparent fluid with a known and controlled refractive index. 

Finally, an important constraint for the methods exposed in this review concerns the optical accesses of the experiment. All these methods rely on the use of at least one camera above or below the surface. Refraction based methods as the FS-SS proposed by Moisy et al. 2008 seem to be among the easiest to set up because only a camera and a printed pattern are requested. However, this method is limited by the curvature of the waves related to the water height. This 

limitation has been treated by Fouras et al. 2008 or Gomit et al. 2013 using a reference camera under the tank and a laser sheet. However, these methods require wide optical accesses to the test facilities. 

## **6  Conclusion and perspectives** 

The review of optical surface measurement methods highlights the large number of methods available in the literature. These non-intrusive methods have been grouped under 3 categories. As discussed in the review, each method has advantages and limitations that must be considered when selecting a measurement method. However, in a large part of the presented studies, the experimental constraints related to the application (size of the field, accessibility, wave properties) have motivated the authors to develop new methods or to adapt existing methods. 

These developments continue today with a continuously evolving literature such as the recent papers of Li et al. 2021 and Steinmann et al. 2021. In this last study, the authors developed a coupled measurements of interface topography and three-dimensional velocity field of a free surface flow based on volumetric LPT technique (Fig. 21). This type of technique, that provides the evolution of the free surface and the three-dimensional velocity field, is promising because it allows the evaluation of the pressure field based on known boundary conditions and the energy balance. 

In the fluid mechanics community, the use and development of data assimilation (DA) approaches is a fast growing topic (see for example Meldi and Poux 2017 and the DA challenge: Schroeder et al. 2020). DA can be used to combine experimental data and computational methods to enhance the prediction of flow fields. The use of such approaches based on field surface data represents an interesting development area. An example of the use of DA with 2015. In this free surface flows is provided in Combès et al. study, the authors reconstructed the free surface flow dynamics of a shallow water model using noisy measurement. 

Finally, many commercial sensors are now available to perform 3D surface measurements. These sensors, based on stereovision methods or three-dimensional LIDAR, are mainly adapted to solid surface measurements and more
