# **Modified free‑surface synthetic Schlieren method to adjust measurement sensitivity in high‑strain waves** 

## **Hillel Mermelstein[1] · Yuval Dagan[1]** 

Received: 31 May 2024 / Revised: 26 August 2024 / Accepted: 3 November 2024 / Published online: 10 December 2024 © The Author(s) 2024 

## **Abstract** 

This research revisits the free-surface synthetic Schlieren technique (FS-SS) (Moisy et al. in Exp Fluids 46(6):1021–1036, 2009) for the topographical measurement of the free surface of a liquid. In contrast to the aforementioned method, which utilizes the image refraction of a random dot pattern through a free surface to determine the surface gradients, this present study mathematically derives a method where the pattern may take an arbitrary three-dimensional shape. This is shown to be theoretically valid under a small pattern slope approximation. Our method is then verified against the previously mentioned, flat-pattern, free-surface synthetic Schlieren technique by resolving the free-surface elevation of plane waves across a channel, showing similar results in constant strain conditions, with improved results in variable strain conditions, particularly in cases where there is a very large difference in strain across the channel. The validation test cases investigated include a rectangular channel containing a transparent liquid with a random dot pattern placed below at a constant angle and a pattern placed on top of a cosine-shaped profile. Both of these setups are validated against the classical FS-SS technique involving a flat pattern. The new method involving an arbitrarily shaped pattern proposed here may increase the resolution in low-amplitude regions by increasing the surface–pattern distance below these regions and correspondingly reducing the sensitivity in high-strain regions by decreasing the surface–pattern distance. Geometries shown to produce advantageous results in waves that include both regions of very high strains and regions of very low amplitudes are explored, resolving the wave in both regions simultaneously. This shows promise in resolving multi-scale surface waves in highly viscous liquids, which may include very high-amplitude regions quickly followed by very low-amplitude regions due to damping effects.

There are other methods to measure the gradients of a fluid surface, such as the gradient detector (Zhang and Cox 1994), which encodes the gradient via a lit-up colored pattern placed well below the fluid surface, which refracts through the deformed fluid. We expect to resolve the wave further along the channel (at smaller wave amplitudes) than the flat case. The difference in results between the first and third sets of experiments stems from the use of different fluids. The height map of the air gap, _ha_ ( _x_ , _y_ ) , was simple to measure for the constant angled pattern as well as the cosine-shaped pattern. For more complex geometries of dot patterns, care should be taken to correctly map out _ha_ ( _x_ , _y_ ) . The heights in each case are reconstructed by numerically integrating the gradient using the `intgrad2` function (D’Errico 2024) as is done in Moisy et al. (2009).

The FS-SS technique, as presented by Moisy et al. (2009), has been explored and modified to a case of arbitrary pattern distance. This was shown to be theoretically valid under the same assumptions applied in Moisy et al. (2009), namely the paraxial, weak slope, and weak amplitude approximations, with the addition of the weak pattern slope approximation, which requires that for any pattern, the slope of the pattern should remain small. This was then verified by comparing the reconstructed surface heights of plane waves obtained via a constant angle pattern and cosine-shaped pattern against a flat pattern. These produce similar results, giving more confidence to the validity of the theory. It was then shown to decrease the measured strains, which is a significant limitation of the classical method. Additionally, it proves beneficial in certain cases with a large damping effect by increasing the surface–pattern distance and, in turn, the resolution in the region where the wave height is smallest. 

This method seems appropriate to resolve the heights of waves across very large channels, where, like the highly viscous case discussed here, the amplitude begins very high but eventually decreases below the scope of the resolution. By angling the pattern, these long waves may be resolved all at once across a longer distance. This method may also prove useful in resolving plane waves traveling over barriers, where the amplitude may drastically change across the barrier.

## **Appendix B: Effect of integration reference point** 

In Moisy et al. (2009), the author discusses a limitation of the FS-SS method in that it produces an artificial change in height of an unperturbed surface when imaging an incoming wave. This phenomenon is displayed in the spacetime diagram in Fig. 18a, which is constructed by taking the fluid height in the center across the channel. Similar to the other setups investigated in this study, this wave is produced by propagating a 4 Hz wave on a bath of water, with a mean depth of 2 mm. The pattern is placed flat with respect to the fluid at rest. 

As the incoming wave enters the viewing window, the height of the unperturbed area of the fluid surface artificially increases. The cause of this is the integration step of the process. Gomit et al. (2022) point out that for methods in which the surface gradient is measured, such as the FS-SS method, and the height is determined via integration, the absolute height of the surface cannot be calculated unless a reference height is known. In practice, this is done by choosing a point on the reconstructed height map and setting it to a known height value. In Fig. 18a, the point chosen is one at the beginning of the channel, where the wave enters from. This is erroneously set to a constant value of zero, which produces streaks in the data. Not only is this reference height incorrect, but the true height also oscillates over time, causing an unwanted periodic shift in the reconstructed height. To address this issue, the reference point is chosen at the end of the channel (shown in Fig. 18b), where the wave has not yet reached and where damping effects are most pronounced. Here, the constant 

value is correctly set to zero, the actual height of the wave at this point, and it remains constant over time due to damping effects, eliminating the unwanted artifact.
